## Layer: Base (Scenario Setup)

**File:** `voiceover_1.7_base.mp3`
**Trigger:** Auto-play on slide load
**Tone:** Storytelling, drawing the learner in

(Beat — 1.5 seconds)

Let us put what you have learned into practice with a scenario.

(Beat — 1 second)

You are working at the front desk when Mrs. Okafor arrives. She is 68 years old, and she looks frustrated. She explains that she has been trying to use the new online booking system for three days, but she cannot navigate it. The system meets accessibility standards — it has been tested for screen reader compatibility and colour contrast. But Mrs. Okafor has limited digital literacy. The interface is confusing to her. She has come in person because she could not figure it out on her own.

(Beat — 2 seconds)

Now it is your turn. Think about the Accessibility Decision Path. What should you do?

Choose from the three options on screen. Take your time — there is no penalty for choosing an imperfect answer. This is about learning.

## Layer: Choice A — Best Response

**File:** `voiceover_1.7_choiceA.mp3`
**Trigger:** On selection of Choice A
**Tone:** Affirming, warm

You chose to apologize for the difficulty, book her appointment right away, and let her know she can always call or come in person. You also note the issue for your team lead.

(Beat — 1 second)

This is the best response. Mrs. Okafor feels heard. She gets her appointment without further frustration. And by flagging the issue, you are helping the system improve for everyone. You addressed the immediate need and the systemic barrier — both matter.

## Layer: Choice B — Acceptable Response

**File:** `voiceover_1.7_choiceB.mp3`
**Trigger:** On selection of Choice B
**Tone:** Balanced, constructive

You chose to book her appointment and suggest she ask a family member to help next time.

(Beat — 1 second)

This is an acceptable response — Mrs. Okafor gets her appointment, which is the immediate priority. But suggesting she rely on family puts the burden of solving the problem on her. The systemic issue — that the booking system is technically accessible but not truly usable for everyone — goes unreported.

Think about the Decision Path. Step five is Seek Support — in this case, that means reporting the barrier upstream.

**[85 words | ~34 seconds]**

## Layer: Choice C — Poor Response

**File:** `voiceover_1.7_choiceC.mp3`
**Trigger:** On selection of Choice C
**Tone:** Gentle but direct

You chose to tell her the system is accessible and offer to walk her through it on your computer.

(Beat — 1 second)

This response misses the mark. While the system may meet technical accessibility standards, "accessible" and "usable" are not the same thing. Mrs. Okafor has already tried for three days. Walking her through it again does not address the real barrier — which is that the system does not work for her needs.

Remember: meeting technical standards is not enough. Accessibility means offering multiple ways to access services. Phone, in person, online — the choice should be the patient's.

**[98 words | ~39 seconds]**

## Layer: Debrief

**File:** `voiceover_1.7_debrief.mp3`
**Trigger:** After any choice feedback, on "Continue" click
**Tone:** Reflective, connecting to frameworks

(Beat — 1 second)

The key lesson here: accessibility includes usability. Meeting technical standards is important, but it is not the end of the story. Always offer multiple pathways, and report barriers upstream — even when the system has been certified as accessible.

This scenario connects to the Accessibility in Practice model — specifically, the Response area. Take timely, respectful action to remove barriers.
