I have been coming to UHN for seven years. I use a power chair. I have a communication disability that means I sometimes need extra time to get my words out. I have had hundreds of appointments across four UHN sites.

I can tell you the exact moment I know whether an appointment is going to go well or badly. It is not the clinical part. It is the first 30 seconds at intake.

It is the tone of voice. When someone at the desk looks up, makes eye contact, and says "Hi, how can I help you?" — not fast, not performative, just present — that changes everything. I relax. I know I am going to be heard. The whole visit shifts.

But when someone does not look up, or talks past me to whoever I am with, or starts reaching for my wheelchair handles without asking — I tighten up. I am already calculating how much energy this visit is going to cost me. And that is before anyone has taken my blood pressure.

Here is something I wish every staff member knew: asking is not a burden. It is the single best thing a clinician ever did for me. One nurse, years ago, before adjusting anything — the bed height, the gown, the blood pressure cuff — she just said: "What works best for you?" Five words. And then she listened to the answer. She did not assume she knew. She did not rush. She confirmed what I said back to me: "So you prefer the cuff on your left arm, and you would rather stay in your chair for vitals — did I get that right?"

That was it. That was the whole interaction. Five words, then a confirmation. And it was the best clinical experience I have ever had at UHN. Not because of the diagnosis or the treatment plan. Because she asked, and then she checked.

The thing is, most staff want to do the right thing. They are not unkind. They are busy. They are under pressure. And when you are busy, you default to the fastest path — which usually means assuming what the patient needs instead of asking. But assuming is where dignity breaks down.

If I could give every person at UHN one sentence to use on a busy shift, it would be this: "Is there anything I should know to make this easier for you?" That is it. You do not need training. You do not need a policy manual. You just need that sentence, and the willingness to listen to the answer.

Dignity starts before the appointment. It starts at the front desk. It starts with your tone of voice. And it costs nothing.
