# Accessibility Decision Path
## Quick Reference

**UHN Accessibility First | Guide 01**

---

**[BODY — Five numbered steps in a vertical flow, each with icon, title, description, and example]**

### Step 1: PAUSE

**What to do:** Stop before you act. Notice what is happening. Check your assumptions.

**Ask yourself:**
- Am I making an assumption about what this person needs?
- Is there a barrier I have not noticed?
- Do I need to slow down before responding?

**Example:** A patient approaches your desk and you notice they use a wheelchair. Before you do anything, pause. Do not assume they need help. Wait for them to tell you what they need.

---

### Step 2: LISTEN

**What to do:** Hear the person. Ask them directly how they would like to be supported.

**Ask yourself:**
- Have I asked the person what they need, rather than assuming?
- Am I listening to their words, body language, and communication preferences?
- Am I directing my attention to the person, not their companion?

**Example:** "How can I best assist you today?" or "Do you have a preferred way to receive this information?"

---

### Step 3: APPLY

**What to do:** Use your training, your knowledge of UHN policies, and the resources available to you.

**Ask yourself:**
- What have I learned in the Accessibility First series that applies here?
- What UHN policies or resources are relevant?
- What has worked in similar situations before?

**Example:** You know that Video Remote Interpreting (VRI) is available for Deaf patients. You access the VRI system to support a patient who communicates in ASL.

---

### Step 4: ADAPT

**What to do:** If your first approach does not work, adjust. Flexibility is a strength.

**Ask yourself:**
- Is my current approach working for this person?
- What could I do differently?
- Can I offer an alternative format, method, or pathway?

**Example:** You offered a printed form in large print, but the patient says they prefer to receive information verbally. You read the form aloud and confirm their understanding.

---

### Step 5: SEEK SUPPORT

**What to do:** If you are unsure, ask for help. You do not need to have all the answers.

**Who to contact:**
- Your manager or charge nurse
- UHN Patient Relations: [insert internal extension]
- Accessibility resources: [insert internal resource link]
- Interpreter services: [insert internal extension]

**Example:** A patient has a disability you have not encountered before. You are not sure how to adapt your approach. You call Patient Relations for guidance while ensuring the patient is comfortable and informed.

---

**[FOOTER — Navy background, white text]**

**Remember:** Pause. Listen. Apply. Adapt. Seek Support.

Accessibility is not about having all the answers. It is about having a process.

# Models of Disability
## Understanding the Difference

**UHN Accessibility First | Guide 01**

---

**[BODY — Three-column comparison table]**

|  | Medical Model | Social Model | Human Rights Model |
|--|---------------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Core idea** | Disability is a problem in the individual that needs to be fixed, treated, or cured. | Disability is caused by barriers in the environment and society, not by the individual. | People with disabilities have fundamental rights to full participation, dignity, and equity in all aspects of life. |
| **Focus** | Fix the person. | Fix the barriers. | Ensure full participation. |
| **Who is responsible?** | The individual and their medical team. | Society, institutions, and policymakers. | Everyone — individuals, institutions, and systems working together. |
| **Language used** | "Suffering from," "confined to a wheelchair," "handicapped." | "Experiences barriers," "uses a wheelchair," "disabled by the environment." | "Has the right to," "entitled to access," "participates fully." |
| **Healthcare example** | A Deaf patient is told to bring their own interpreter. The hospital treats the communication gap as the patient's problem. | The hospital installs visual alert systems and removes communication barriers, but does not actively ensure the patient can participate in care decisions. | The hospital ensures ASL interpretation is available, provides information in accessible formats, involves the patient in care planning, and continuously improves systems to prevent future barriers. |
| **Strength** | Recognizes that medical treatment is important. | Shifts responsibility from the individual to the environment. | Centres dignity, equity, and systemic accountability. Recognizes intersectionality. |
| **Limitation** | Frames disability as a deficit. Can lead to paternalism, exclusion, and stigma. | Can underestimate the role of impairment-related experiences (e.g., pain, fatigue). | Requires sustained institutional commitment and cultural change. |
| **Legal basis in Ontario** | Not reflected in current accessibility legislation. | Partially reflected in the AODA (barrier removal). | Fully reflected in the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), which Canada ratified. Aligns with the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, Section 15. |

---

**[CALLOUT BOX — Lilac background, navy text]**

### At UHN, we use the Human Rights Model.

This means we do not just accommodate — we include. We do not just remove barriers — we actively ensure participation, dignity, and equity for every person.

---

**[FOOTER — Navy background, white text]**

**Key takeaway:** How you think about disability shapes how you respond. The human rights model is our foundation.

### Content — Front

**[HEADER — Navy background, white text, compact]**

# Accessibility First
## Guiding Principles

---

**[BODY — Five principles, each with icon placeholder and one-line description]**

**1. Dignity**
Treat every person with respect. Honour their autonomy, preferences, and expertise about their own needs.

**2. Independence**
Support people to do things for themselves. Do not take over. Offer assistance — do not impose it.

**3. Inclusion**
Design systems, spaces, and processes with disability in mind from the start — not as an afterthought.

**4. Proactive Barrier Removal**
Do not wait for someone to encounter a barrier. Look for barriers before they become problems.

**5. Equity**
Recognize that equal treatment is not always equitable. Different people need different supports to achieve the same outcomes.

---

### Content — Back

**[HEADER — Cobalt background, white text]**

## Putting Principles into Practice

---

**Quick Self-Check (use before, during, or after any interaction):**

- [ ] Did I treat this person with dignity?
- [ ] Did I support their independence without taking over?
- [ ] Is this space/process designed to include people with disabilities?
- [ ] Have I looked for barriers proactively, or am I waiting for someone to complain?
- [ ] Am I providing equitable support — not just equal treatment?

---

**When in doubt, use the Decision Path:**

**Pause** -- **Listen** -- **Apply** -- **Adapt** -- **Seek Support**
