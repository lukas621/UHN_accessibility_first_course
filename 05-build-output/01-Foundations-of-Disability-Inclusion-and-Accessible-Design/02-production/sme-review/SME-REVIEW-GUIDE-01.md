## Instructions for Reviewers

Thank you for reviewing this guide. Your expertise is essential to ensuring the accuracy, relevance, and inclusiveness of this content for UHN staff.

**How to use this document:**

1. Each review question is organized by risk level (Critical, High, Medium, Low).
2. For each question, a content excerpt is provided so you can review in context without needing to read the entire guide.
3. Please provide your response directly below each question or use the comment function if reviewing digitally.
4. If you identify issues not covered by these questions, please add them at the end in the "Additional Reviewer Comments" section.

**Risk Levels Defined:**

| Risk Level | Definition | Impact if Incorrect |
|------------|------------|---------------------|
| Critical | Legislative interpretation, Indigenous content, patient safety | Legal liability, cultural harm, regulatory non-compliance |
| High | Scenario accuracy, policy interpretation, clinical practice depiction | Misinformation, learner confusion, credibility damage |
| Medium | Terminology, practice tips, assessment item accuracy | Reduced learning effectiveness, minor inaccuracies |
| Low | General wording, UI labels, formatting | Cosmetic issues, minor clarity improvements |

## Medium Risk Questions (7-9)

### Question 7: Models of Disability Terminology

**Suggested Reviewer:** Disability Studies Scholar or Accessibility Educator

**Content Excerpt (from Assessment Bank, MT-01):**

> "Medical Model: Disability is a deficit or deviation within the individual that requires diagnosis, treatment, or cure."
>
> "Social Model: Disability is caused by societal barriers and exclusionary design, not by the individual's impairment." (Oliver, 1990)
>
> "Rights-Based Model: People with disabilities are rights-holders entitled to full and equal participation in society."

**Review Question:** Are these descriptions of the three models accurate, appropriately nuanced, and current? Is the attribution to Oliver (1990) for the social model sufficient, or should additional scholars be cited? Is there a risk that the medical model description could be perceived as dismissive of legitimate clinical interventions? Should a note be added clarifying that these models are not mutually exclusive in practice?
