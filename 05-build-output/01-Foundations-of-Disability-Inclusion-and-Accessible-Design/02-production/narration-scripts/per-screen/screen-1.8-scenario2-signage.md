Here is a second scenario.

(Beat — 1 second)

You notice a patient with low vision squinting at a directional sign in the outpatient clinic. The sign was recently installed and meets Ontario Building Code standards, but the font is small and it is mounted high on the wall. The patient asks you for directions to the lab.

(Beat — 2 seconds)

Think about the Decision Path — Pause, Listen, Apply, Adapt, Seek Support.

Choose from the options on screen. The best response addresses both the immediate need and the systemic barrier.

## Layer: Choice A — Best Response

**File:** `voiceover_1.8_choiceA.mp3`
**Trigger:** On selection of Choice A
**Tone:** Affirming

You chose to walk the patient to the lab, then report the signage issue to facilities with a suggestion to add larger, lower signs with high-contrast text.

(Beat — 1 second)

This is the best response. You addressed the patient's immediate need — getting to the lab — while also acting on the systemic barrier. By reporting to facilities with a specific suggestion, you are helping prevent this problem for the next person.

**[66 words | ~26 seconds]**

## Layer: Choice B — Acceptable Response

**File:** `voiceover_1.8_choiceB.mp3`
**Trigger:** On selection of Choice B
**Tone:** Balanced, constructive

You chose to give clear verbal directions and offer to walk the patient partway.

(Beat — 1 second)

This is helpful — the patient finds the lab. But the signage problem remains for the next person. Think about the Decision Path — Step five is Seek Support. Reporting the barrier to facilities turns a one-time fix into a permanent improvement.

**[56 words | ~22 seconds]**

## Layer: Choice C — Poor Response

**File:** `voiceover_1.8_choiceC.mp3`
**Trigger:** On selection of Choice C
**Tone:** Gentle but direct

You chose to point to the sign and read it aloud.

(Beat — 1 second)

This response singles the patient out and draws attention to their difficulty without actually solving the problem. The patient may feel embarrassed. The barrier is not addressed. And reading a sign aloud does not help someone navigate a complex hospital.

Remember the Accessibility in Practice model — Environment means ensuring spaces are accessible. Response means acting with dignity.

**[66 words | ~26 seconds]**

**Tone:** Reflective

(Beat — 1 second)

The key lesson: meeting technical standards does not guarantee practical accessibility. Consider accessibility from the user's perspective. And always report barriers — do not just work around them.

This connects to two guiding principles: Proactive Barrier Prevention and Independence and Autonomy.
