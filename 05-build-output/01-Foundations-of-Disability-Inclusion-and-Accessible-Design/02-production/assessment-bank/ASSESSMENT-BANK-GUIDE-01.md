## Multiple Choice Items (MC-01 through MC-04)

### MC-01

**Linked CLO:** CLO 1
**Bloom's Level:** Understanding (Level 2)
**Item Type:** Multiple Choice -- Single Select

**Stem:** A patient who uses a power wheelchair arrives at a UHN outpatient clinic and can enter the building independently using the automatic doors. However, the clinic's patient intake forms are only available on paper at a high counter, and the receptionist speaks rapidly without checking comprehension. Which statement best describes the barriers this patient is experiencing?

A) The patient is experiencing physical barriers because the counter is too high.
B) The patient is experiencing communication and systemic barriers, even though the physical environment is partially accessible.
C) The patient is not experiencing any barriers because they were able to enter the building.
D) The patient is experiencing attitudinal barriers caused by the receptionist's personal bias against people with disabilities.

**Correct Answer:** B

**Feedback -- Correct:** Correct. Accessibility extends beyond physical access. This patient encounters communication barriers (rapid speech without comprehension checks, paper-only forms at an inaccessible height) and systemic barriers (intake processes that were not designed with diverse needs in mind). Recognizing these multiple dimensions is essential for truly inclusive healthcare.

**Feedback -- Incorrect:** Not quite. While the high counter is a physical element, the broader picture includes how information is communicated and how organizational systems were designed. Accessibility involves physical, attitudinal, systemic, communication, digital, and policy dimensions. Re-read the scenario and consider which processes and interactions create barriers beyond the built environment.

### MC-02

**Linked CLO:** CLO 2
**Bloom's Level:** Knowledge (Level 1)
**Item Type:** Multiple Choice -- Single Select

**Stem:** Under the Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act (AODA), which of the following is a requirement that applies to UHN as a public-sector healthcare organization?

A) Provide accessibility training only to front-line clinical staff who interact with patients directly.
B) Ensure that all employees and volunteers receive training on the Integrated Accessibility Standards Regulation (IASR) and the Ontario Human Rights Code as it pertains to people with disabilities.
C) Comply with accessibility standards only when a patient files a formal complaint.
D) Meet accessibility standards for new construction but not for existing facilities or digital services.

**Correct Answer:** B

**Feedback -- Correct:** Correct. The AODA and its Integrated Accessibility Standards Regulation (IASR) require that all employees, volunteers, and anyone who provides goods, services, or facilities on behalf of the organization receive training on accessibility standards and on the Human Rights Code as it relates to people with disabilities (O. Reg. 191/11, s. 7). This applies to all staff, not only those in patient-facing roles.

**Feedback -- Incorrect:** The AODA requires comprehensive accessibility training for all employees and volunteers, not only front-line staff. Compliance is proactive and ongoing, not complaint-driven, and extends to digital services and existing processes -- not just new construction. Review the IASR training requirements under O. Reg. 191/11, section 7.

### MC-03

**Linked CLO:** CLO 4
**Bloom's Level:** Analysis (Level 4)
**Item Type:** Multiple Choice -- Single Select

**Stem:** A UHN department receives a request from a Deaf patient for a sign language interpreter for an upcoming appointment. The department arranges an interpreter within 48 hours. Which of the following best characterizes this response?

A) Proactive inclusive design, because the department responded promptly.
B) Reactive accommodation, because the service was arranged only after a specific request was made.
C) Universal design, because the interpreter service is available to all patients.
D) Systemic inclusion, because the department has a process for handling such requests.

**Correct Answer:** B

**Feedback -- Correct:** Correct. While the department responded appropriately, this is reactive accommodation -- a response triggered by an individual request. Proactive inclusive design would involve embedding interpreter availability, multilingual signage, and communication options into the standard appointment process so that patients do not need to disclose a disability and advocate for support each time they seek care.

**Feedback -- Incorrect:** A prompt response is important, but responding only after a patient requests an accommodation is reactive by definition. Proactive design would build interpretation services, visual communication tools, and multiple format options into the default service delivery model. Consider what the appointment experience would look like if no request were needed at all.

### MC-04

**Linked CLO:** CLO 5
**Bloom's Level:** Application (Level 3)
**Item Type:** Multiple Choice -- Single Select

**Stem:** During a team huddle on a medical-surgical unit at UHN, a nurse mentions that a patient with a cognitive disability seems confused by the whiteboard schedule in their room. A colleague responds, "That is not really our responsibility -- the accessibility office handles that." Which response from the charge nurse best reflects shared organizational responsibility?

A) "You are right. Submit a request to the accessibility office and they will follow up."
B) "Let us flag it for now and revisit at the next quarterly accessibility audit."
C) "Actually, accessibility is all of our responsibility. Let us think about what we can do right now to help this patient understand their schedule, and also flag the whiteboard design for broader review."
D) "We should wait for the patient's family to arrive and explain the schedule to them."

**Correct Answer:** C

**Feedback -- Correct:** Correct. Shared organizational responsibility means that every team member, regardless of role, has a part to play in identifying and reducing barriers. The charge nurse's response addresses the immediate patient need while also initiating a systemic improvement -- both hallmarks of an accessibility-first culture.

**Feedback -- Incorrect:** Accessibility is a shared responsibility across all roles and levels, not solely the domain of a specialized office. Waiting for audits or family members delays resolution and places the burden on the patient. Effective accessibility practice involves both immediate action and systemic follow-up. Review the concept of shared organizational responsibility in the guiding principles.

## Multiple Select Items (MS-01 and MS-02)

### MS-01

**Linked CLO:** CLO 1
**Bloom's Level:** Understanding (Level 2)
**Item Type:** Multiple Select (select all that apply)

**Stem:** Which of the following are recognized dimensions of accessibility barriers in healthcare? Select ALL that apply.

A) Physical barriers (e.g., stairs, narrow doorways, inaccessible examination tables)
B) Attitudinal barriers (e.g., assumptions about a patient's capacity based on their disability)
C) Financial barriers experienced only by uninsured patients
D) Communication barriers (e.g., lack of plain language materials, no sign language interpretation)
E) Digital barriers (e.g., inaccessible patient portals, websites that do not work with screen readers)
F) Policy barriers (e.g., intake procedures that require handwritten signatures only)

**Correct Answers:** A, B, D, E, F

**Feedback -- Correct:** Correct. Accessibility barriers span physical, attitudinal, communication, digital, and policy dimensions. While financial barriers are real, option C narrowly frames them as affecting only uninsured patients, which is inaccurate -- financial barriers can affect anyone. The five dimensions listed in the correct answers reflect the comprehensive understanding of accessibility emphasized in this guide.

**Feedback -- Incorrect:** Review the five dimensions of accessibility barriers covered in this guide: physical, attitudinal, communication, digital, and policy. Option C is too narrow in its framing. Each of the other options represents a recognized category of barrier that healthcare organizations must address.

### MS-02

**Linked CLO:** CLO 3
**Bloom's Level:** Analysis (Level 4)
**Item Type:** Multiple Select (select all that apply)

**Stem:** A 72-year-old Cantonese-speaking woman with low vision is navigating a UHN hospital to attend a diagnostic imaging appointment. Which of the following factors illustrate how intersectionality can compound barriers to access? Select ALL that apply.

A) Wayfinding signage is in English and French only, with small font sizes.
B) The patient portal sends appointment reminders only by email, in English.
C) The diagnostic imaging department is located on the ground floor with step-free access.
D) Staff assume the patient's adult child should make decisions on her behalf.
E) The patient's Ontario Health Insurance Plan (OHIP) covers the imaging procedure.

**Correct Answers:** A, B, D

**Feedback -- Correct:** Correct. Intersectionality means that this patient's experience is shaped by the interaction of multiple identity factors -- age, language, disability, and cultural background. Signage that is not multilingual or in large print (A), digital-only English communications (B), and ageist assumptions that remove her agency (D) all illustrate compounding barriers. Step-free access (C) and OHIP coverage (E) are facilitators, not barriers.

**Feedback -- Incorrect:** Consider how multiple aspects of this patient's identity -- age, language, disability status, and cultural background -- interact to create compounding barriers. Options C and E describe positive facilitators rather than barriers. Review the intersectionality framework (Crenshaw, 1989) and how it applies to healthcare access.

## Scenario-Based Items (SB-01 and SB-02)

### SB-01

**Linked CLO:** CLO 4
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluation (Level 5)
**Item Type:** Scenario-Based (constructed response with rubric)

**Scenario:** You are a program coordinator at a UHN ambulatory care clinic. Your clinic recently transitioned to a fully digital check-in system using touchscreen kiosks. Since implementation, several patients -- including an older adult with essential tremor, a patient with low vision, and a newcomer to Canada with limited English proficiency -- have reported difficulty using the kiosks. The clinic manager states, "We meet AODA compliance because the kiosk vendor certified the product as accessible."

**Prompt:** In 200-300 words, evaluate the clinic manager's statement. In your response: (a) explain why vendor certification alone may not ensure accessibility in practice, (b) identify at least two specific barriers the kiosk system creates for the patients described, and (c) recommend two changes that would shift the clinic from reactive accommodation to proactive inclusive design.

**Rubric:**

| Criterion | Exemplary (3) | Proficient (2) | Developing (1) | Insufficient (0) |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Critical analysis of compliance vs. accessibility | Clearly distinguishes between technical compliance and real-world usability; identifies systemic assumptions embedded in the kiosk-only approach | Distinguishes compliance from usability but with limited depth | Mentions the distinction but does not develop the analysis | Does not address the distinction |
| Barrier identification | Identifies 2+ specific barriers with clear connections to the patients described | Identifies 2 barriers but connections to patients are vague | Identifies 1 barrier | Does not identify specific barriers |
| Proactive design recommendations | Recommends 2+ actionable changes that embed inclusion into default processes | Recommends 2 changes but they remain reactive in nature | Recommends 1 change | Does not provide recommendations |
| Use of course concepts | Integrates relevant concepts (e.g., reactive vs. proactive, dimensions of accessibility, AODA/OHRC principles) accurately | References course concepts but with minor inaccuracies | Mentions a concept without applying it | Does not reference course concepts |

### SB-02

**Linked CLO:** CLO 3 and CLO 5
**Bloom's Level:** Application (Level 3)
**Item Type:** Scenario-Based (constructed response with rubric)

**Scenario:** You are a clinical educator at UHN's Toronto Western Hospital. During an in-service training session on patient communication, a physiotherapist says: "I treat everyone the same -- I do not see disability. That way I am being fair to all my patients." Another team member, an occupational therapist, responds: "But some of our patients face more barriers than others because of who they are -- not just their diagnosis."

**Prompt:** In 150-250 words: (a) explain why the "treat everyone the same" approach, while well-intentioned, can perpetuate inequity for patients with disabilities who hold additional marginalized identities, and (b) describe one concrete action the physiotherapy team could take to move toward equitable, intersectional practice.

**Rubric:**

| Criterion | Exemplary (3) | Proficient (2) | Developing (1) | Insufficient (0) |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Analysis of equity vs. equality | Clearly explains why identical treatment is not equitable; connects to intersectionality framework | Explains the distinction but does not connect to intersectionality | Mentions equity or equality without analysis | Does not address the distinction |
| Concrete action | Proposes a specific, actionable, and feasible team-level practice that reflects intersectional awareness | Proposes an action that is relevant but vague | Proposes an action unrelated to intersectionality | Does not propose an action |
| Use of inclusive language and course concepts | Writing demonstrates inclusive, person-centred language and integrates course terminology accurately | Mostly inclusive language with minor lapses; references some course concepts | Language or concepts are inconsistently applied | Language is exclusionary or no course concepts are used |

## Reflection Prompts for MAP Activity (RP-01 and RP-02)

### RP-01

**Linked CLO:** CLO 5
**Bloom's Level:** Synthesis/Creation (Level 6)
**Item Type:** Reflection Prompt (My Accessibility Plan -- MAP)

**Prompt:** Think about your current role at UHN. Identify one process, practice, or interaction in your daily work that may unintentionally create a barrier for a patient, visitor, or colleague with a disability. Describe the barrier, explain which dimension(s) of accessibility it involves (physical, attitudinal, systemic, communication, digital, or policy), and outline one realistic action you could take in the next 30 days to begin addressing it. Consider how your action connects to the principle of shared organizational responsibility.

**Guidance for Facilitators:** This reflection feeds into the learner's My Accessibility Plan (MAP), which they will build across all guides in the series. Responses are not graded but should be reviewed for engagement depth. Look for: specificity to the learner's role, correct identification of barrier dimensions, feasibility of the proposed action, and connection to shared responsibility. Encourage learners to revisit and update this entry as they progress through subsequent guides.

## Matching Item (MT-01)

### MT-01

**Linked CLO:** CLO 1 and CLO 4
**Bloom's Level:** Understanding (Level 2)
**Item Type:** Matching

**Instructions:** Match each model of disability with its defining characteristic and implication for healthcare practice.

**Column A -- Model**

1. Medical Model
2. Social Model
3. Rights-Based Model

**Column B -- Defining Characteristic and Implication**

A) Disability is caused by societal barriers and exclusionary design, not by the individual's impairment. Healthcare practice should focus on removing environmental and systemic barriers so that people with disabilities can participate fully. (Oliver, 1990)

B) Disability is a deficit or deviation within the individual that requires diagnosis, treatment, or cure. Healthcare practice focuses on fixing or managing the individual's condition to approximate "normal" functioning.

C) People with disabilities are rights-holders entitled to full and equal participation in society. Healthcare practice must uphold dignity, autonomy, and non-discrimination as legal and ethical obligations, consistent with the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) and the Ontario Human Rights Code (OHRC).

**Correct Matches:**

| Model | Match |
|-------|-------|
| 1. Medical Model | B |
| 2. Social Model | A |
| 3. Rights-Based Model | C |

**Feedback -- Correct:** Correct. Understanding these three models is foundational. The medical model locates disability in the individual; the social model locates it in societal barriers; and the rights-based model frames disability as a matter of human rights and legal entitlement. In practice at UHN, a rights-based approach informed by the social model guides us to design inclusive environments and uphold the dignity and autonomy of every patient, visitor, and team member.

**Feedback -- Incorrect:** Review the three models: the medical model focuses on the individual's impairment as the "problem"; the social model (Oliver, 1990) shifts the focus to barriers created by society; and the rights-based model (UN CRPD; OHRC) centres the legal and ethical rights of people with disabilities to full participation. Consider which model places responsibility on the environment and systems rather than on the person.
