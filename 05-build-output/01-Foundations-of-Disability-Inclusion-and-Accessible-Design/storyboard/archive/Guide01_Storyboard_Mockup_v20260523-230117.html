SECTION 01 · PURPOSE

Accessibility is not an extra. It is care.

Progressive reveal · static with audio

This guide introduces shared language, foundational accessibility practices, and awareness of barriers within healthcare environments. It is aligned with the Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act (AODA), the Ontario Human Rights Code (OHRC), and UHN Accessibility Policy.

It establishes a common foundation for understanding accessibility as both a legal obligation and a core component of safe, equitable, and person-centred care.

1 in 5
Ontarians have a disability. Accessibility failures increase legal exposure, patient safety risk, and institutional reputational harm.
STRATEGIC INTENT

Establish accessibility as a proactive, system-level governance responsibility embedded in leadership, policy, and operational decision-making.

WHY THIS GUIDE MATTERS

Accessibility failures increase legal exposure, patient safety risk, employee disengagement, and institutional reputational harm. This Guide creates shared language and accountability foundations for the full series.

CALLOUT

This series is dignity-based, not compliance-driven.

SECTION 01 · CROSS-CUTTING

Accessibility includes cultural safety.

Static with audio · mandatory 30s dwell

Accessibility in Canada must also be understood within the broader context of colonial history and its ongoing impacts on First Nations, Inuit, and Métis Peoples. These impacts include intergenerational trauma, systemic discrimination, and inequities in access to health services, geography, and culturally appropriate care, including language and communication supports.

Accessibility includes respecting Indigenous rights to self-determination, cultural practices, and ways of knowing. This includes recognizing the role of family, community, and language in care and decision-making.

Practicing accessibility alongside cultural safety, humility, and respect for Indigenous rights supports more equitable, trustworthy, and person-centred care.

Guide 01 · Foundations › Understanding Disability
SECTION 02 · CONTEXT

Disability is broad — and it is not a problem to be fixed.

Disability includes a wide range of physical, sensory, mental health, intellectual, learning, and non-visible experiences.
01

Disability

A condition that may interact with barriers to limit participation.
Disability is defined by the interaction between a person and the barriers in their environment. It is not a characteristic of the person alone. Click to collapse.
02

Impairment

A medical or functional condition.
An impairment refers to the physical, sensory, cognitive, or mental health condition itself. It does not automatically mean the person is disabled — disability arises when barriers exist. Click to collapse.
03

Handicap

An outdated term referring to disadvantage created by environments. We do not use this term.
This term placed the disadvantage within the individual. Modern practice recognizes the environment as the source of barriers. Use "disability" or "barrier" instead. Click to collapse.
TYPES

Visible · Non-visible · Episodic

PEOPLE FIRST & DIGNITY

Refer to people before conditions; avoid defining people by diagnosis; focus on participation, not deficits.

Guide 01 · Foundations › Models of Disability
SECTION 02 · MODELS

Three models — three very different practices.

Disability exists along a continuum of concepts, both nationally and internationally. The term has no universally accepted definition.
1

Medical Model

Initially, disabilities were viewed primarily through a medical lens, focusing on deficiencies and often placing blame on the individual.
The medical model treats disability as a personal deficit to be "fixed." While medical intervention can be important, this model alone ignores environmental and social factors. Click to collapse.
2

Social Model

As societal awareness grew, the focus shifted to the contributions of people with disabilities, emphasizing the social and environmental barriers that lead to their exclusion.
The social model says disability is created by barriers in the environment, not by the individual. Removing barriers — not "fixing" people — is the goal. Click to collapse.
3

Rights Model

A strong emphasis on the rights of people with disabilities, asserting they are entitled to the same rights as everyone else, regardless of the nature or severity of their disabilities. Supported by the UN CRPD.
The rights model builds on the social model by asserting that access is a fundamental human right. It is the foundation of the UN CRPD and the framework used by Accessibility First. Click to collapse.
CONTINUUM

This progression reflects a shift from deficit-focused approaches to rights-based, inclusive practices. Accessibility First works in the rights model.

SECTION 02 · THE FOUR AREAS — USED ACROSS ALL 18 GUIDES

The Accessibility in Practice Model

Accessibility is most effective when applied in everyday interactions and decisions through four interconnected areas.
01

Awareness

Recognize that accessibility needs may not be immediately visible. Be mindful of bias, assumptions, and systemic barriers.
  • Scan environments and forms.
  • Notice assistive devices.
  • Watch your own assumptions.
Awareness is the foundation. Before you can act, you need to notice. Practice scanning your environment for barriers, and check your own assumptions about who has needs and what those needs might be.
02

Communication

Listen, ask, and adapt communication to understand and respond to individual needs, preferences, and cultural context.
  • Ask about preferred format.
  • Speak to the patient, not the companion.
  • Slow down when needed.
Communication is two-way. Always speak directly to the person with the disability — not to their companion. Ask how they prefer to receive information, and be prepared to adapt on the spot.
03

Environment

Ensure that physical, sensory, and social environments are accessible, inclusive, and safe.
  • Clear paths and seating.
  • Lighting and signage.
  • Quiet space if needed.
The environment includes physical space, lighting, noise, signage, temperature, and social atmosphere. Many barriers are environmental and can be addressed without waiting for a request.
04

Response

Take timely and respectful action to remove or reduce barriers.
  • Offer — don't impose — help.
  • Confirm what worked.
  • Update the chart or note.
Response means acting on what you have noticed and heard. Offer support without imposing it. After, confirm the outcome, document it, and close the loop so the next team member knows what works.
REFLECTION

The four areas are not a checklist. They are four lenses you can move between in any interaction — sometimes within the same minute.

SCENARIO 2

Physical Environment

A clinic installs signage that meets technical accessibility standards for placement and design. However, the signs are mounted high on the wall and use small font sizes that are difficult for some patients with low vision or mobility challenges to read.

Patients frequently ask for directions or rely on others for assistance navigating the space. This demonstrates that meeting technical standards does not always translate into accessible everyday experiences.

Inclusive Practice Tip: Consider accessibility from the user's perspective. Use clear fonts, adequate contrast, and place signage at heights that are easily visible and readable for people with diverse mobility and vision needs.

SECTION 06 · RISK

Why Governance Matters

Four institutional risks of accessibility failure.
RISK 01

Human Rights Tribunal Exposure

Failure to accommodate can result in complaints to the Human Rights Tribunal of Ontario, with financial and reputational consequences.

Awards can include financial compensation, policy changes, and mandatory training. Cases are public and create reputational risk.
RISK 02

Constructive Dismissal Risk

When employees with disabilities are denied accommodation, the working relationship may be fundamentally breached.

A failure to accommodate an employee can constitute constructive dismissal even without termination, exposing the organization to legal liability.
RISK 03

Patient Safety Incidents

Inaccessible systems contribute to communication failures, medication errors, and missed care needs.

When communication or physical barriers prevent a patient from understanding instructions or accessing care, patient safety is directly compromised.
RISK 04

Complaint Escalation Patterns

Unaddressed barriers create patterns that escalate to formal complaints, media attention, and institutional investigations.

A single unresolved complaint can grow into a pattern. Tracking and resolving accessibility concerns early prevents escalation to external bodies.
CLOSING

Accessibility must be embedded in governance — not delegated informally. If a situation feels high-risk, follow the Escalation Pathways on the next screen.

SECTION 06 · ESCALATE

Where to Take It Next

Five escalation paths. When in doubt — start with Manager + People and Culture.
Hub-and-spoke flow diagram — click any card to expand
👤 YOU
1. Manager
First line; everyday questions and ongoing barriers
Your direct manager is the first point of contact for day-to-day accessibility questions, workplace adjustments, and ongoing barriers within your team. They can authorize immediate changes and escalate systemic issues.
2. People & Culture
HR accommodation, employee experience, complaints
People & Culture handles formal accommodation requests, employee experience concerns, workplace complaint patterns, and policy-level HR interventions. They coordinate with managers and external providers.
3. IDEAA Office
Cultural safety, intersectional concerns, policy alignment
The IDEAA (Inclusion, Diversity, Equity, Accessibility & Anti-Racism) Office addresses cultural safety questions, intersectional concerns, and ensures alignment with organization-wide equity and accessibility policies.
4. Patient Relations
Patient complaints, feedback, resolution
Patient Relations manages patient-facing complaints, feedback, and resolution requests related to accessibility. They ensure patient concerns are documented, tracked, and resolved in a timely manner.
5. Risk & Legal
Human Rights Tribunal, patient safety, media risk
Risk & Legal handles high-stakes situations: Human Rights Tribunal exposure, patient safety incidents, and media risk. Escalate here when the situation involves legal liability or reputational risk to the organization.
