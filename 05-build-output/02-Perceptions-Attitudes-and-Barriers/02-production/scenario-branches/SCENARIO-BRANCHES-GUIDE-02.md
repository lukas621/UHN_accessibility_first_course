## Scenario Design Rules

- Each scenario: realistic UHN/Ontario healthcare context
- 3 choices per scenario: Best, Acceptable, Poor
- Each choice: immediate consequence + debrief line
- Feedback is non-punitive — all choices get a learning response
- Correct choice is not labelled "correct" in the UI — just consequence text
- After debrief, learner continues to next screen (no loop back unless additional activity)
- Person-first language throughout; cultural representation intentional and reviewed

## Scenario 1 — Assumptions About Ability (Screen 2.10)

**Setting:** Hospital reception desk, outpatient clinic — UHN

**Character:** Mr. Santos, patient using a manual wheelchair. He is a 52-year-old man, fluent in English, waiting for an intake appointment. He has his intake form on the armrest of his chair.

**Protagonist role:** You are a UHN staff member passing by the unattended reception desk.

**Setup (exact on-screen text):**
You are walking past the reception desk on your way to another task. Mr. Santos, a patient using a manual wheelchair, has been waiting for 10 minutes. There are no staff at the desk and no sign indicating when someone will return. His intake form is on his armrest — he has not started it yet.

**Question:** What do you do?

---

### Choice A — Best

**On-screen label:** "Introduce yourself and ask how you can help."

**Learner action:** You stop, introduce yourself, and ask: "Good morning — I can see you have been waiting a while. Is there anything I can do to help you while someone comes to the desk?"

**Consequence:**
Mr. Santos thanks you and says he is fine with the form but would appreciate knowing when someone will be back. You let him know and flag the desk gap to your supervisor. He completes his form independently and feels respected. The desk coverage issue is noted for follow-up.

**Debrief:**
This is the dignity-centred response. You offered help without assuming it was needed, you gave Mr. Santos the information he asked for, and you reported a systemic issue. One interaction — three accessibility principles in action.

**Principle:** People First & Dignity — Independence & Autonomy — Proactive Barrier Prevention

---

### Choice B — Acceptable

**On-screen label:** "Apologize for the wait and fill in the form for him."

**Learner action:** You stop, apologize for the wait, take the form from his armrest, and begin filling it in by asking him questions as you go.

**Consequence:**
Mr. Santos answers your questions but feels a little uncomfortable. He did not ask for the form to be taken — he was about to start it himself. The task gets completed, but his sense of control over his own intake has been reduced. His experience of the interaction is mixed.

**Debrief:**
This response is helpful and well-intentioned — but it skips the essential first step: asking. Taking the form without permission mirrors the original attitudinal barrier (paternalism) even while trying to address the wait time. The fix is one question: "Would you like help with the form, or would you prefer to fill it in yourself?"

**Principle:** People First & Dignity — Independence & Autonomy

---

### Choice C — Poor

**On-screen label:** "Move the form to the desk and tell him someone will be there soon."

**Learner action:** You pick up the form from his armrest, set it on the desk, tell him someone will be back soon, and continue on your way.

**Consequence:**
Mr. Santos watches you move his form without asking. He has to wait even longer, is now further from his form, and feels dismissed. The interaction leaves him feeling that the system was not designed for him — and confirms a pattern he has experienced before.

**Debrief:**
This response prioritizes your own task over the patient's experience — and it involves taking something from him without asking. Even a brief pause and a simple question would have made a significant difference. Accessibility is often built in seconds.

**Principle:** People First & Dignity — Independence & Autonomy

## Scenario 2 — Implicit Bias in the Workplace (Screen 2.11)

**Setting:** Team office or meeting room, UHN administrative or clinical unit

**Characters:** You are a team leader. Priya, a healthcare team member, disclosed a mood disorder 18 months ago and took a medical leave. She has since returned and has received consistently positive performance reviews. Another equally qualified candidate, Marcus, has applied for the same role.

**Setup (exact on-screen text):**
You are assigning the lead role on a new quality improvement project. Both applicants — Priya and Marcus — have strong, comparable performance reviews. You know that Priya disclosed a mood disorder last year and took a medical leave. Her work since returning has been excellent. You need to decide now.

**Question:** What do you do?

---

### Choice A — Best

**On-screen label:** "Assign based on merit and recent performance."

**Learner action:** You review both candidates' most recent performance data. You find that the evidence supports either candidate, and you make the decision based on the specific competencies the role requires. You do not factor in Priya's medical history.

**Consequence:**
Priya is assessed equitably. Whether she is selected or not, the process is fair. If she is selected and later needs support, she and you can have that conversation then — with her input, not without it. The team observes that disclosed disability did not result in career limitation.

**Debrief:**
This is the equitable response — and the legally defensible one. Under the OHRC, a disability history cannot be used to limit professional opportunity. The duty to accommodate is triggered when a person requests accommodation, not when a manager decides in advance that someone needs protection.

**Principle:** Equity, Rights & Intersectionality — People First & Dignity

---

### Choice B — Acceptable

**On-screen label:** "Assign Priya to a supporting role to reduce her workload."

**Learner action:** Concerned about the potential pressure on Priya, you assign her to a supporting rather than lead role — without telling her your reasoning. You tell both candidates the decision was based on fit.

**Consequence:**
Priya accepts the role but notices she was passed over. She does not know the reason. Over time, she may connect the decision to her disclosure and feel that being honest about her health cost her a career opportunity. This can reduce disclosure and damage psychological safety across your team.

**Debrief:**
This decision was made "for" Priya, without her input or knowledge. Paternalism in professional contexts is both a dignity issue and a legal risk. If you had concerns about workload, the right step was to have a direct conversation with Priya — not to make a decision based on an assumption about her limits.

**Principle:** People First & Dignity — Equity, Rights & Intersectionality

---

### Choice C — Poor

**On-screen label:** "Assign Marcus because the workload may be too much for someone who has taken medical leave."

**Learner action:** You assign Marcus, reasoning that you cannot risk the project being disrupted if Priya's health becomes a factor again.

**Consequence:**
This decision is based directly on an assumption about Priya's future capacity, rooted in her disability history. Priya finds out through team channels. She files a complaint with HR. An OHRC review begins. The decision is found to constitute discrimination on the basis of disability.

**Debrief:**
This is a textbook example of how implicit bias becomes discriminatory action. The assumption — "her health may become a factor again" — is not a performance observation. It is a prediction based on disability. The OHRC prohibits this. Good intent does not shield a decision from scrutiny when the basis for it is disability status.

**Principle:** Equity, Rights & Intersectionality

## Scenario 3 — Cultural Misunderstanding in Care (Screen 2.12)

**Setting:** Inpatient unit or outpatient pre-procedure assessment room, UHN

**Characters:** You are a nurse conducting a pre-procedure assessment. Mrs. Nguyen is an 82-year-old Vietnamese-Canadian patient. Her adult daughter, Linh, is present as a support person.

**Setup (exact on-screen text):**
You are conducting a pre-procedure assessment for Mrs. Nguyen. She is quiet, avoids direct eye contact, and gives short, indirect answers. Her daughter Linh is present and responds more fully when you ask questions. Mrs. Nguyen does not appear confused — she follows the conversation and nods at appropriate moments.

**Question:** What do you do?

---

### Choice A — Best

**On-screen label:** "Continue addressing Mrs. Nguyen directly, and invite Linh's input after."

**Learner action:** You slow your pace, use plain and respectful language, and continue addressing questions to Mrs. Nguyen. After she has had time to respond, you acknowledge Linh: "Thank you, Linh. Is there anything you would like to add?" You give Mrs. Nguyen control of the conversation.

**Consequence:**
The assessment takes a few extra minutes, but it is accurate. Mrs. Nguyen nods and eventually offers a key piece of information about her discomfort during the last procedure — something Linh did not know. The clinical record is more complete. Mrs. Nguyen feels respected.

**Debrief:**
This approach honours both the cultural dynamic and the patient's primacy. Indirect communication, deference to family, and avoiding direct eye contact are not signs of confusion or non-compliance — they are communication styles. Adapting your approach without displacing the patient's voice is the inclusive response.

**Principle:** Inclusion & Integration — People First & Dignity — Communication (AiP)

---

### Choice B — Acceptable

**On-screen label:** "Shift to directing questions to Linh for efficiency."

**Learner action:** You recognize that Linh communicates more directly and begin directing your questions to her instead. You keep Mrs. Nguyen in your peripheral awareness and check in with her occasionally.

**Consequence:**
The assessment moves faster. But Mrs. Nguyen becomes quieter as the interaction shifts away from her. Some of her direct observations — about pain levels and concerns — are filtered through Linh's interpretation. The clinical record reflects Linh's account, not fully Mrs. Nguyen's experience.

**Debrief:**
Efficiency is a reasonable goal — but not at the cost of the patient's voice. When we route communication through a family member, we risk missing clinical information and reducing the patient's sense of agency. The inclusive approach takes a few more minutes and produces more accurate, patient-centred care.

**Principle:** People First & Dignity — Inclusion & Integration

---

### Choice C — Poor

**On-screen label:** "Document that the patient is unable to provide history and proceed."

**Learner action:** After two attempts to engage Mrs. Nguyen, you note in the chart that the patient "unable to provide history" and proceed with Linh as the primary informant.

**Consequence:**
Mrs. Nguyen's cultural communication style has been misread as a clinical incapacity. The documentation is inaccurate and potentially harmful — it may lead future providers to assume cognitive impairment. If a care decision is made based on this note, the error compounds. A complaint is filed.

**Debrief:**
This is a clinical documentation error with patient safety implications. "Unable to provide history" describes a clinical incapacity — not a communication style difference. Misrecording this can affect every future interaction Mrs. Nguyen has with the healthcare system. The question to ask was: "How can I create the conditions for this person to be heard?" — not "Can she communicate?"

**Principle:** People First & Dignity — Equity, Rights & Intersectionality

## Scenario 4 — Language and Mental Health in Handoff (Screen 2.13)

**Setting:** Nursing station, inpatient unit, UHN

**Characters:** You are Amir, an incoming nurse starting a shift. The outgoing nurse, David, is giving handoff. Mr. Thompson is a 45-year-old patient in room 4B with a bipolar disorder diagnosis noted in his chart.

**Setup (exact on-screen text):**
During shift handoff, David says: "Room 4B — Mr. Thompson. He can be difficult. There's a mental health history. Just be aware." He points to the chart entry noting bipolar disorder. You have not yet met Mr. Thompson. The handoff is otherwise brief.

**Question:** What do you do as Amir?

---

### Choice A — Best

**On-screen label:** "Acknowledge the handoff and meet Mr. Thompson without assumptions."

**Learner action:** You thank David for the handoff, note the clinical information, and go to introduce yourself to Mr. Thompson with a neutral, open manner. "Good morning, Mr. Thompson. I am Amir — I will be your nurse for this shift. How are you feeling today?" You let his actual behaviour — not David's description — guide your approach.

**Consequence:**
Mr. Thompson is measured and cooperative. He asks a thoughtful question about his medication schedule. Amir realizes the "difficult" label bore no resemblance to his actual experience. He flags the handoff language issue to the charge nurse at end of shift.

**Debrief:**
Every patient deserves a fresh start. A clinical diagnosis does not predict behaviour. When we carry forward a colleague's attitudinal framing — without our own observation — we often create the very dynamic we expected. Amir's approach models professional curiosity over bias transfer.

**Principle:** People First & Dignity — Proactive Barrier Prevention

---

### Choice B — Acceptable

**On-screen label:** "Flag the handoff language to the charge nurse before entering the room."

**Learner action:** You recognize that "he can be difficult" is not clinical information. Before going in, you stop at the charge nurse's station: "I want to flag something from handoff — the description of 4B felt like an attitude transfer rather than clinical context. Can you give me more information about his care needs?"

**Consequence:**
The charge nurse confirms that Mr. Thompson has had some challenging interactions in the past but offers specific clinical context — not a personality label. Amir enters with better information and without bias. The charge nurse begins thinking about how to address handoff language with the team.

**Debrief:**
This is a thoughtful, professional response. It delays the care interaction briefly but produces better clinical context and begins addressing the systemic handoff language issue. If Amir had the clinical context to recognize the problem, this approach models advocacy — not just personal practice.

**Principle:** Proactive Barrier Prevention — Inclusion & Integration

---

### Choice C — Poor

**On-screen label:** "Enter the room already guarded and watchful."

**Learner action:** David's description has you on edge. You enter room 4B with less warmth than usual, keep interactions brief, and brace for pushback. When Mr. Thompson asks a question you find slightly assertive, you interpret it through the lens of "difficult."

**Consequence:**
Mr. Thompson senses the difference immediately. He becomes more guarded in response. By the end of the shift, the interaction has been tense — matching the prediction. Amir files a note that Mr. Thompson "appeared guarded and resistant." The cycle continues for the next nurse.

**Debrief:**
This is a self-fulfilling prophecy driven by transferred bias. The label "difficult" has now shaped two clinical encounters and a piece of documentation. This is how attitudinal barriers reproduce in healthcare systems — not through a single bad actor, but through the uncritical transfer of framing from one person to the next. The antidote is awareness and a willingness to begin fresh.

**Principle:** People First & Dignity — Proactive Barrier Prevention
