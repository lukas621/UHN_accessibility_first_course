## Question 1 — Knowledge Check 1 (Screen 2.14)

**CLO Mapping:** CLO 1 — Define attitudinal barriers and explain how implicit and explicit bias affect accessibility and equity in healthcare

**Bloom's Level:** Understand

**Question Stem:**
Which of the following BEST describes implicit bias?

**Options:**

| Option | Text | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | A deliberate belief that a group of people is inferior | No |
| B | An unconscious attitude that shapes behaviour without awareness | **Yes** |
| C | A policy that intentionally excludes people with disabilities | No |
| D | A formal complaint about discriminatory treatment | No |

**Correct Answer:** B

**Feedback — Option A (Incorrect):**
"This describes explicit bias — a conscious, deliberate attitude that a person is aware of holding. Implicit bias is different: it operates below the level of awareness, which is what makes it particularly challenging to address. Review Screen 2.7."

**Feedback — Option B (Correct):**
"That is right. Implicit bias is unconscious — it shapes our behaviour and judgements without our being aware of it. That is what makes reflective practice so important. By building the habit of noticing and naming our reactions, we can begin to catch implicit bias before it causes harm."

**Feedback — Option C (Incorrect):**
"This describes systemic or structural discrimination — bias embedded in policies or practices. Implicit bias operates at the individual level, below the level of conscious awareness. Both are important to address, but they are distinct concepts."

**Feedback — Option D (Incorrect):**
"A formal complaint is a response to discrimination after it has occurred — not a type of bias. Implicit bias is an unconscious attitude. Review Screen 2.7 for the distinction between implicit and explicit bias."

## Question 2 — Knowledge Check 2 (Screen 2.15)

**CLO Mapping:** CLO 2 — Recognize how assumptions about ability, culture, mental health, and identity influence patient care and employee interactions

**Bloom's Level:** Apply/Analyze

**Question Stem:**
A nurse automatically speaks more slowly and loudly to a patient using a power wheelchair, even though there is no documented hearing or cognitive concern in the chart. This is an example of:

**Options:**

| Option | Text | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Explicit bias — the nurse is deliberately patronizing the patient | No |
| B | Spread effect — assuming one disability affects other areas of functioning | **Yes** |
| C | Invisibilization — dismissing the patient's non-visible disability | No |
| D | Paternalism — deciding what the patient needs without asking | No |

**Correct Answer:** B

**Feedback — Option A (Incorrect):**
"The nurse is likely unaware that this behaviour is occurring — this is the hallmark of implicit, not explicit, bias. Explicit bias requires conscious awareness and intent. The specific mechanism at work here is the spread effect, which operates unconsciously."

**Feedback — Option B (Correct):**
"Exactly right. The spread effect is the assumption that one disability automatically affects other areas of functioning. Using a wheelchair does not indicate hearing loss or cognitive differences. This nurse's behaviour, though likely well-intentioned, communicates a harmful and inaccurate assumption. The patient deserves to be addressed normally."

**Feedback — Option C (Incorrect):**
"Invisibilization involves dismissing or doubting a disability that is not visibly apparent — for example, telling someone with chronic pain that they 'look fine.' This scenario involves the opposite: over-attributing limitation to a visible disability. That is the spread effect."

**Feedback — Option D (Incorrect):**
"Paternalism involves making decisions on someone's behalf without their input — for example, taking a patient's form without asking. This scenario involves an assumption about ability, not a care decision. The specific barrier type is the spread effect."

## Question 3 — Knowledge Check 3 (Screen 2.16)

**CLO Mapping:** CLO 4 — Demonstrate proactive, dignity-centred responses that move accessibility practice beyond compliance

**Bloom's Level:** Analyze/Evaluate

**Question Stem:**
Under the Ontario Human Rights Code, a manager who withholds a leadership opportunity from an employee because of a disclosed disability — without the employee's input — is most likely:

**Options:**

| Option | Text | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Acting appropriately by protecting the employee from stress | No |
| B | Fulfilling the duty to accommodate | No |
| C | Committing a form of paternalism that may constitute discrimination | **Yes** |
| D | Complying with AODA requirements | No |

**Correct Answer:** C

**SME Note:** Legal accuracy requires review by OHRC/HR legal counsel before final publication.

**Feedback — Option A (Incorrect):**
"Protecting someone from stress by limiting their opportunities — without consulting them — is not a supportive action. It is a form of paternalism that removes the employee's autonomy and career agency. Under the OHRC, this type of unilateral decision may constitute discrimination on the basis of disability."

**Feedback — Option B (Incorrect):**
"The duty to accommodate requires a collaborative process involving the employee. It does not authorize managers to make unilateral decisions about what an employee can or cannot handle. Withholding opportunities without the employee's knowledge or input does not fulfill this duty — it undermines it."

**Feedback — Option C (Correct):**
"That is right. Making decisions 'for' someone based on a disability assumption — without their participation — is both paternalistic and potentially discriminatory under the OHRC. Accommodation is a conversation, not a decision made about someone. The employee must be involved in any adjustments to their role or responsibilities."

**Feedback — Option D (Incorrect):**
"The AODA addresses accessibility in the provision of goods, services, and employment practices — such as accessible formats and communication supports. It does not authorize withholding opportunities. The relevant framework here is the OHRC Human Rights Code and the duty to accommodate."

## Alternate / Reserve Questions (for future use or adaptive tracks)

### Reserve Q4 — Intersectionality

**CLO:** CLO 2
**Bloom's Level:** Analyze

**Stem:** A patient who is Indigenous, Deaf, and living in poverty presents with multiple access needs. The concept that best describes the combined and compounding effect of these factors is:

| Option | Text | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Multiple diagnosis | No |
| B | Intersectionality | **Yes** |
| C | Comorbidity | No |
| D | Complex care needs | No |

**Correct (B):** "Intersectionality describes how multiple aspects of a person's identity — race, disability, socioeconomic status — interact to shape their experience in ways that cannot be reduced to any single factor. This concept is essential for equitable, person-centred care."

**Incorrect (A):** "Multiple diagnosis is a clinical term referring to more than one health condition. Intersectionality is a broader social and political framework about how identity factors compound experiences of discrimination and access."

**Incorrect (C):** "Comorbidity refers to the co-occurrence of two or more clinical conditions. Intersectionality addresses the compounding effect of social identity factors — race, disability, poverty — on a person's experience of access and discrimination."

**Incorrect (D):** "'Complex care needs' is a clinical descriptor, not a framework for understanding how identity factors interact. Intersectionality is the concept that captures this dynamic."

### Reserve Q5 — Reflective Practice

**CLO:** CLO 3
**Bloom's Level:** Apply

**Stem:** During a patient interaction, a healthcare worker notices they feel impatient with a patient who is taking a long time to communicate. What is the BEST first step in the Notice-Name-Examine-Act cycle?

| Option | Text | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Apologize to the patient and ask a colleague to take over | No |
| B | Pause internally and acknowledge the feeling of impatience | **Yes** |
| C | Remind yourself of the AODA requirements | No |
| D | Document the interaction as "communication challenge" | No |

**Correct (B):** "The first step is to Notice — to catch yourself in the moment and acknowledge the reaction without judgment. You cannot examine or change what you have not first acknowledged. This internal pause is where reflective practice begins."

**Incorrect (A):** "Handing off the patient is a behavioural response, not a reflective one. It also removes the opportunity to practice a more equitable interaction. The cycle begins with noticing and naming what is happening internally."

**Incorrect (C):** "Recalling compliance requirements is a cognitive exercise that skips the reflective step. The cycle begins with noticing the emotion or assumption that arose — in this case, impatience — and examining where it comes from."

**Incorrect (D):** "Documenting a 'communication challenge' without first examining your own reaction risks attributing the barrier entirely to the patient. The reflective cycle asks you to notice your own response first."
