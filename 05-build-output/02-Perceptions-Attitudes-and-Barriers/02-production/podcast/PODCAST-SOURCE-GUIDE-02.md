## Source Material for Discussion

### Opening Hook (0:00–2:00)

Have you ever walked into a room already having a story about the person you are about to meet?

Most of us would say no. Most of us would be wrong.

In healthcare, this matters more than almost anywhere else. Because the "story" we carry about a patient — their diagnosis, their appearance, their manner of speaking — shapes how we interact with them before a single word is exchanged.

Today we are talking about what happens when our perceptions and attitudes become the barrier. Not a narrow doorway. Not a missing ramp. Not a policy gap. Us.

---

### The Science of Implicit Bias (2:00–5:30)

Implicit bias is not a moral failure. It is a cognitive feature.

The human brain takes shortcuts. Faced with more sensory information than it can consciously process, it categorizes, clusters, and predicts based on prior experience. This is adaptive. In most of life, it is helpful.

In healthcare, it can be harmful.

Research published in the *American Journal of Public Health* (Hall et al., 2015) found that most healthcare providers showed implicit bias based on race, weight, age, and disability — often without awareness.

Implicit bias in clinical settings has been linked to:
- Differential pain assessment (lower pain scores assigned to Black patients and patients with mental health diagnoses)
- Communication differences (shorter interactions, less information shared)
- Referral disparities (fewer specialist referrals for certain patient groups)
- Diagnostic delays (conditions dismissed or attributed to psychiatric history)

The question is not whether bias exists. It is whether we can develop the awareness to interrupt it before it shapes a clinical decision.

### Attitudinal Barriers — The Six Types (5:30–9:00)

There are six attitudinal barrier types worth naming because naming them is the first step in interrupting them.

**Paternalism** — Deciding what is best for someone without asking. In healthcare, this looks like taking over a task a patient was handling, making referral decisions based on assumed limitations, or offering an accommodation no one asked for.

**Stereotyping** — Applying group-based assumptions to individuals. Every person who uses a wheelchair is different. Every person with a bipolar diagnosis is different. The moment we respond to the category instead of the person, we have activated a stereotype.

**The Pity or Charity Model** — Viewing people with disabilities through a lens of tragedy or inspiration. "Wheelchair warrior." "Despite their challenges, they..." This framing locates the person's value in their relationship to their disability, not in who they are.

**The Spread Effect** — Assuming one disability affects all areas of functioning. A person with a visual impairment may have exceptional verbal recall. A person with a cognitive disability may communicate preferences with perfect clarity. Assuming otherwise misses critical clinical information.

**Othering** — Treating people with disabilities as fundamentally different — as objects of curiosity, pity, or special regard rather than as patients and colleagues. "I don't even think of you as disabled." This is meant as a compliment. It is not.

**Invisibilization** — Dismissing or doubting non-visible disabilities. "You don't look sick." "Everyone gets tired sometimes." When disclosures are met with skepticism, people stop disclosing. And when people stop disclosing, barriers go unaddressed.

### The "Difficult Patient" Phenomenon (9:00–12:30)

In clinical handoffs across every hospital in Canada, nurses, physicians, and support staff pass a phrase from shift to shift that does more harm than almost any other piece of informal information: "difficult patient."

"Difficult patient" is not a clinical descriptor. It does not describe a symptom, a behaviour, or a care need. It describes an attitudinal residue — the leftover impression one provider had and chose to hand off to the next.

What happens when a nurse enters a room having been told the patient is "difficult"?

- They are shorter in their interactions.
- They interpret ambiguous behaviour through a negative lens.
- They document observations that confirm the prediction.
- The next handoff carries the same label — now with "consistent" evidence.

This is called labelling theory, and it operates in healthcare with real clinical consequences. Patients labelled as difficult receive fewer explanations, experience more rushed interactions, and are less likely to have their concerns taken seriously.

The alternative is not ignoring clinical history. It is the discipline to enter every interaction with a fresh question: "What do I actually observe in this person today?"

### Intersectionality: The Compounding Effect (12:30–15:30)

Disability does not exist in a vacuum.

A patient who is Indigenous, elderly, and hard of hearing navigates healthcare differently than a patient who is white, middle-class, and recently disabled. Their disability is the same. Their experience is not.

Intersectionality — a framework developed by legal scholar Kimberlé Crenshaw — describes how multiple aspects of identity interact to shape a person's experience of systemic barriers. Race, gender, disability, income, language, age — these factors do not add up; they multiply.

In Ontario, this has specific significance:

**Indigenous Peoples** face the compounding effects of colonial history, geographic barriers, systemic racism in healthcare settings, and cultural disconnection from care models built on Western biomedical frameworks. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission's Calls to Action (18–24) address health equity directly.

**Racialized Canadians with disabilities** report higher rates of unmet healthcare needs, differential diagnostic treatment, and communication barriers.

**Newcomers and refugees** navigating the healthcare system with cognitive or communication disabilities may lack language access, cultural mediators, and familiarity with systems that already disadvantage them.

Inclusive practice is not one-size-fits-all. It is the disciplined practice of asking: "Who is the person in front of me — not who do I expect them to be?"

### Reflective Practice: The Notice-Name-Examine-Act Cycle (15:30–17:30)

The research is clear: awareness alone does not change behaviour. What changes behaviour is a structured habit of pausing.

Reflective practice in healthcare has been studied extensively. Nurses who engage in structured reflection report:
- Greater awareness of bias in clinical decisions
- Improved therapeutic relationships with patients from different backgrounds
- Reduced burnout (reflecting builds professional identity and purpose)

The Notice-Name-Examine-Act cycle is a four-step habit loop for everyday practice:

1. **Notice** — Catch the moment. "I noticed I felt impatient." "I noticed I assumed."
2. **Name** — Label it without judgment. Not "I am a bad person." "I had an assumption."
3. **Examine** — Where did this come from? What impact could it have?
4. **Act** — Choose a different response based on what you actually observe.

This cycle does not require a journal, a supervisor, or a reflective seminar. It requires 10 seconds and a decision to look.

### Closing Reflection Prompt for Listener

Think of a time when someone held a different assumption about you than the reality — at work, in a healthcare setting, or in daily life. How did it feel to be seen through that lens instead of your own?

Now turn that experience around. Whose lens are you looking through when you walk into the room?

That is where the work begins.
