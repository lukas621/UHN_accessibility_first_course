## Purpose of This Guide

This guide explores how perceptions, attitudes, and assumptions influence accessibility in healthcare settings. It examines the impact of unconscious bias, stereotypes, and cultural assumptions on patient and employee experiences.

The guide supports reflective and inclusive practice by strengthening awareness of how attitudes and behaviours can create or reinforce barriers. It positions accessibility as both an individual and systemic responsibility that extends beyond compliance.

This includes recognizing how historical and systemic factors shape perceptions, interactions, and access to care and services. This guide supports practical, inclusive approaches that can be applied in everyday interactions and decision making. All guides are grounded in Accessibility First principles and informed by a health equity and Indigenous cultural safety lens to support equitable and culturally safe practices.

---

## Indigenous Peoples and Accessibility

Accessibility in Canada must also be understood within the broader context of colonial history and its ongoing impacts on First Nations, Inuit, and Métis Peoples. These impacts include intergenerational trauma, systemic discrimination, and inequities in access to health services, geography, and culturally appropriate care, including language and communication supports.

Accessibility includes respecting Indigenous rights to self-determination, cultural practices, and ways of knowing. This includes recognizing the role of family, community, and language in care and decision-making.

Practicing accessibility alongside cultural safety, humility, and respect for Indigenous rights supports more equitable, trustworthy, and person-centred care. It also requires recognizing how historical and systemic factors, including colonialism, shape experiences of disability and access.

## Section 1: Understanding Bias and Assumptions

Bias — conscious (explicit) or unconscious (implicit) — can influence patient care, employee interactions, and organizational decisions. Assumptions may relate to ability, language, culture, mental health, age, or other identity factors.

**Guiding Principle in Practice:** People First and Dignity

- Check assumptions before acting
- Ask, rather than assume, what a patient or colleague needs
- Approach each interaction with curiosity and respect

**Practice Reflection:** Where have I noticed assumptions influencing patient care or employee interactions?

---

### Scenario 1: Assumptions About Ability

A patient using a wheelchair has been waiting at the reception desk for 10 minutes. No employees are present, and there is no sign indicating when someone will return. When an employee walks by, they take the patient's paperwork without asking and provide directions. The patient feels their autonomy has been undermined, assuming the employee believed they could not manage the task themselves.

**Reflection:** How can employees ensure they respect patient autonomy and avoid assumptions about ability?

**Inclusive Practice Tip:** Always ask before taking over. Offer assistance but respect the patient's independence and choice in how they manage tasks.

---

### Scenario 2: Implicit Bias

A team leader hesitates to assign leadership responsibilities to an employee with a mental health condition, despite clear evidence of competence and experience. This hesitation limits opportunities and reinforces stigma.

**Reflection:** How can employees recognize and challenge implicit bias to support equitable professional development?

**Inclusive Practice Tip:** Focus on skills and performance, not assumptions about a condition. Use objective criteria for assignments and provide equitable opportunities for all employees.

---

### Scenario 3: Cultural Misunderstandings

A patient expresses discomfort using culturally specific gestures or phrases. An employee misinterprets this as non-compliance and delays care. The patient feels misunderstood and disrespected.

**Reflection:** How can employees better interpret patient behaviour through a culturally aware lens?

**Inclusive Practice Tip:** Pause and ask clarifying questions. Seek to understand cultural context before making assumptions about compliance or behaviour.
