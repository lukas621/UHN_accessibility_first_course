When Attitudes Become Barriers

Attitudinal barriers occur when assumptions, stereotypes, or lack of awareness lead to exclusion — even without intent.

In healthcare, these barriers affect diagnosis, treatment decisions, and whether patients feel safe returning.

  • An employee who speaks over a patient with a cognitive disability.
  • A clinician who assumes a patient with a mental health history cannot make sound decisions.
  • A colleague who speaks to a patient's support person instead of the patient directly.
KEY TAKEAWAY

None of these require bad intent. They require awareness. And awareness is something we can build.

The Weight of Stigma

Mental health stigma in healthcare settings delays help-seeking by an average of 11 years.

For employees with disabilities, fear of disclosure can limit career growth and access to supports.

When we examine our own assumptions about mental health — our language, our reactions, our expectations — we begin to break down stigma from the inside out.

KEY TAKEAWAY

Stigma is one of the most powerful attitudinal barriers we know of. At UHN, this affects both patients and employees.

Compounding Barriers

A person's experience of disability is shaped by race, gender, language, income, age, and culture — not disability alone.

Intersectionality means that disability intersects with multiple identity factors to shape a person's experience in ways that are unique to them.

INDIGENOUS CONTEXT

Indigenous Peoples face compounded barriers rooted in colonial history, systemic racism, and cultural disconnection from care.

KEY TAKEAWAY

At UHN, our responses must be flexible, humble, and informed by the person in front of us — not a category or a checklist.

SCREEN 2.7 · CORE CONCEPT

Two Types of Bias

Bias is not a character flaw — it is a feature of how the brain processes information. Select each tab to explore.
Implicit Bias

Definition: An unconscious attitude or stereotype that affects judgement and behaviour without awareness.

Healthcare example: A nurse automatically speaks louder when addressing a patient using a wheelchair, assuming hearing loss.

Impact: Patients feel patronized; trust erodes. The nurse likely means well — the harm is real regardless.

Explicit Bias

Definition: A conscious attitude, belief, or stereotype that is deliberately held and acted upon.

Healthcare example: A provider believes that patients with mental health histories are "difficult" and delays their intake.

Impact: Direct discrimination; creates an unsafe care environment.

KEY INSIGHT

Both types cause harm. The difference is awareness — and awareness gives us a place to start.

Six Types of Attitudinal Barriers

Click each item to expand. Recognizing the specific form helps you respond more effectively.

Assuming you know better than the person what they need. Often comes from a desire to help — but removes the person's autonomy.

Applying group-based assumptions to individuals. Not every person who is Deaf communicates the same way. Not every person using a wheelchair has the same functional needs.

Viewing people with disabilities as objects of sympathy rather than rights-holders. Sees disability as tragedy rather than recognizing people as agents with full lives.

Assuming one disability affects all aspects of a person's functioning. A person with a visual impairment does not necessarily have cognitive or communication differences.

Treating people with disabilities as fundamentally different rather than as peers and patients — as objects of curiosity or concern rather than as people.

Failing to acknowledge non-visible disabilities or dismissing disclosures. "You don't look sick" is a form of attitudinal barrier. Each of these can be unlearned.

Scenario 1: Assumptions About Ability

DEBRIEF

Autonomy means offering help, not assuming it is needed or wanted. Always ask. "Can I help you with anything?" is the opening. Let the person in front of you direct what happens next.

Branching choice · 3 options · graded
SCENARIO 1

Mr. Santos at Reception

You are walking past the reception desk on your way to another task. Mr. Santos, a patient using a manual wheelchair, has been waiting for 10 minutes. There are no staff at the desk and no sign indicating when someone will return. His intake form is on his armrest — he has not started it yet.

What do you do?
A
Introduce yourself and ask: "Is there anything I can do to help you while someone comes to the desk?" You wait until a colleague arrives or handle the intake yourself.
B
Apologize for the wait, take his form, and start filling it in for him, asking him the questions as you go.
C
Pick up the form and set it on the desk, telling him someone will be with him soon, then continue to your task.

Scenario 2: Implicit Bias in the Workplace

DEBRIEF

The duty to accommodate does not mean making decisions for people. It means removing barriers and having conversations. Assumptions about what someone can handle — without asking — are both a bias and a legal risk.

Branching choice · 3 options · graded
SCENARIO 2

Assigning a Project Lead

You are assigning the lead role on a new quality improvement project. Both applicants — Priya and Marcus — have strong, comparable performance reviews. You know that Priya disclosed a mood disorder last year and took a medical leave. Her work since returning has been excellent. You need to decide now.

What do you do?
A
Review both candidates' recent performance and assign based on merit. Do not factor in Priya's medical history.
B
Assign Priya to a supporting role instead of the lead, intending to protect her from added stress without telling her why.
C
Assign the other candidate because you worry the workload might be too much for someone who has taken medical leave.

Scenario 3: Cultural Misunderstanding in Care

DEBRIEF

Cultural communication differences are not compliance problems. Indirect eye contact, deference to family, and non-linear communication patterns are meaningful and valid. The question to ask is: "How can I create the conditions for this person to be heard?"

Branching choice · 3 options · graded
SCENARIO 3

Mrs. Nguyen's Assessment

You are conducting a pre-procedure assessment for Mrs. Nguyen. She is quiet, avoids direct eye contact, and gives short, indirect answers. Her daughter Linh is present and responds more fully when you ask questions. Mrs. Nguyen does not appear confused — she follows the conversation and nods at appropriate moments.

What do you do?
A
Continue addressing questions to Mrs. Nguyen directly, using plain language and pausing frequently. After she responds, acknowledge Linh: "Thank you — is there anything you would like to add?"
B
Begin directing most questions to Linh for efficiency, while checking in with Mrs. Nguyen occasionally.
C
Document that the patient is "unable to provide history" and proceed with Linh as the primary informant.

SCREEN 2.13 · SCENARIO

Scenario 4: Language and Mental Health in Handoff

DEBRIEF

Diagnostic labels do not predict behaviour. Handoff language like "difficult" transfers bias, not clinical information. Each patient deserves a fresh interaction — informed by clinical facts, not by another person's attitudinal lens.

Branching choice · 3 options · graded
SCENARIO 4

Amir's Shift Handoff

During shift handoff, David says: "Room 4B — Mr. Thompson. He can be difficult. There's a mental health history. Just be aware." He points to the chart entry noting bipolar disorder. You have not yet met Mr. Thompson. The handoff is otherwise brief.

What do you do as Amir?
A
Acknowledge the handoff and note the clinical information, then go in to meet Mr. Thompson with a neutral, open manner. Let his actual behaviour — not David's description — guide your approach.
B
Flag the handoff language to the charge nurse before entering the room, asking for clinical context rather than a personality label.
C
Enter the room already guarded, with less patience and a shorter interaction than you would give other patients.

SCREEN 2.17 · PRACTICE

Five Inclusive Practice Tips

Five practices you can start using right away.
1

Ask, Don't Assume

Before offering help, ask: "Is there anything I can do to support you?" Let the person direct what happens next.
2

Watch Your Language

Replace "suffers from" with "has." Avoid "wheelchair-bound" or "mentally ill." Words like "uses a wheelchair" are more accurate and more respectful.
3

Address the Patient

Direct your questions and eye contact to the patient in front of you — not their companion or family member.
4

Examine Handoff Language

When you hear "difficult patient," ask: "Difficult how? What has been tried?" Transfer clinical information, not attitudinal framing.
5

Notice-Name-Examine-Act

Take the reflective practice cycle with you. One pause can change an interaction entirely.

SCREEN 2.20 · SUMMARY

Key Takeaways

Four things to remember from this guide.
1

Invisible but Deeply Felt

Attitudinal barriers are often invisible to those who hold them — but deeply felt by those who experience them. The most powerful barrier prevention begins with awareness.
2

Bias Is Not a Character Flaw

Implicit bias is not a character flaw — it is a cognitive habit. The difference is whether you choose to notice, name, and adjust.
3

Intersectionality Matters

Disability intersects with race, gender, language, age, and culture. A person's experience cannot be reduced to a single category — only curiosity and willingness to ask.
4

Progress, Not Perfection

Inclusive practice is not about getting it right every time. It is about honesty, curiosity, and a willingness to ask before assuming.
