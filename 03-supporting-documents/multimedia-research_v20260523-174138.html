Multimedia Design Research

Design patterns from MasterClass, Coursera, edX, and Udacity — applied to the UHN Accessibility First Course Series (18 courses)

1. Platform Visual Style Comparison

Each platform has a distinct visual DNA. Here's what to borrow from each.

MasterClass Cinematic. Dark. Premium. Black backgrounds, warm lighting, Netflix-feel
Coursera Clean. Academic. Structured. White space, blue accents, clear hierarchy
edX Serious. Credible. Institutional. Dark teal, structured modules, progress tracking
Udacity Practical. Project-based. Tech-forward. Light gray, cyan accents, hands-on workspace

MasterClass BORROW: Visual Quality

  • Cinematic video with warm, dramatic lighting
  • Dark UI with bright text (premium feel)
  • Netflix-style lesson navigation
  • 10-15 min lessons, documentary pacing
  • Expert instructor as visual focal point
  • B-roll footage for visual storytelling
  • "Aesthetic Usability Effect" — beautiful = more usable
  • Limitation: No interactivity — consumption only

Coursera BORROW: Structure

  • Clean white backgrounds, generous whitespace
  • Blue (#0056D2) brand accent for CTAs
  • Talking head + slide overlay combo
  • Progress indicators with green checkmarks
  • Card-based course navigation
  • In-video quizzes for active recall
  • Peer review for assignments
  • University branding adds credibility

edX BORROW: Accessibility

  • W3C Digital Accessibility Foundations = gold standard
  • Talking head at desk + supplemental visuals
  • Not visually flashy — content-first approach
  • Layered navigation with progress tracking
  • Built-in accessibility: captions, transcripts, alt text
  • Module 6 specifically covers multimedia accessibility
  • Serious institutional tone
  • 16-20 hours across 4 weeks pacing

Udacity BORROW: Hands-on

  • Light gray workspace feel
  • Cyan (#01B3E3) brand accent
  • Project-based with mentor review
  • Video + interactive workspace split-screen
  • Figma, Miro, Whimsical integrations
  • Focus on portfolio-worthy output
  • Short video segments + immediate practice
  • Design system thinking built into curriculum

Recommended Hybrid for UHN Accessibility Course

Combine these elements for a healthcare eLearning context in Storyline 360:

  • From MasterClass: Warm, cinematic photography for scenarios. Premium visual quality. Invest in lighting and composition for any video or photo content.
  • From Coursera: Clean white UI, card-based navigation, progress tracking, in-module knowledge checks.
  • From edX/W3C: Accessibility-first design — captions on everything, transcripts available, keyboard navigable. Content-first, not decoration-first.
  • From Udacity: Action-oriented — every screen has something to DO. MAP activity = Udacity-style project output.

3. Scenario-Based Learning Patterns

How top platforms and award-winning eLearning handle scenarios — the core learning pattern for accessibility training.

Pattern 1: Photo-Based Branching Scenario

Best for: Patient interaction scenarios, reception desk encounters, clinical communication

Visual: Realistic photograph of the scene. Character dialogue appears as text overlay. Learner chooses from 2-3 response options.

Feedback: Consequence-based — shows what happens next based on choice. No "wrong/right" labels, just realistic outcomes.

Platform reference: Elucidat's discrimination/ethics training pattern

Realistic Photo Branching
Pattern 2: Video Branching with Social Polling

Best for: Mental health scenarios, trauma-informed care, de-escalation

Visual: Short video clip sets up scenario. Learner makes a choice. Next video clip shows consequence. Social polling shows "68% of your colleagues chose..."

Feedback: Delayed — learner reflects before seeing what peers chose. Normalizes diverse approaches to complex situations.

Platform reference: Elucidat's mental health support example

Video Branching + Polling
Pattern 3: Interactive Storytelling with Audio

Best for: Service animal encounters, assistive device interactions, communication accessibility

Visual: Illustrated or photo scene with ambient audio. Music clips and sound effects create emotional engagement. No judgment given — learner self-reflects.

Feedback: Suspenseful narrative unfolds before core content delivery. Story-first, teaching-second.

Platform reference: MasterClass storytelling approach adapted for eLearning

Illustration Story-driven
Pattern 4: Simulation with Gamification

Best for: Crisis situations, de-escalation, time-sensitive accessibility responses

Visual: First-person perspective. Timer creates urgency. Points system rewards good accessibility decisions. Leaderboard optional.

Feedback: Immediate score feedback. "You earned 8/10 accessibility points." Debrief screen explains optimal approach.

Platform reference: Udacity project-based approach + gamified eLearning patterns

Gamified Animated UI

Recommended Scenario Approach for UHN

Primary pattern: Photo-based branching (Pattern 1) — most realistic for healthcare, manageable to produce across 18 courses, works well in Storyline 360.

Reserve video branching (Pattern 2) for the most sensitive guides: Guide 6 (Mental Health), Guide 16 (Trauma-Informed), Guide 17 (Crisis/De-escalation).

Use Cathy Moore's Action Mapping methodology — the dominant framework for scenario-based eLearning design. Focus on what learners need to DO, not just KNOW.

4. Disability Representation in Course Imagery

Critical guidelines for a course series ABOUT accessibility — the visuals must model what the content teaches.

Key Principle

For courses teaching accessibility, the imagery IS the curriculum. Stereotypical, pitying, or inaccurate disability representation undermines the entire learning experience. Your visuals must model the behaviors you're teaching.

Disability Representation Checklist (from Disability:IN, Scope UK, AODA)

Use authentic models — feature people who actually have disabilities. People can tell when someone is acting.
Show diversity within disability — no single image represents all disability. Vary disabilities, ages, races, genders across the series.
Avoid patronizing imagery — never show people with disabilities as poor, needy, or helpless. Show confidence and capability.
Normalize, don't dramatize — Scope UK's "End the Awkward" approach. Disability is part of everyday life, not a dramatic event.
Show agency — people making decisions, communicating, participating. Not being helped, pitied, or observed.
Accurate assistive devices — correct wheelchair types, realistic hearing aids, proper white cane technique. Details matter.
Context matters — show people in professional, social, and everyday settings. Not just medical/clinical contexts.
Include non-visible disabilities — Guides 6, 7, 8, 15, 16 cover mental health, intellectual, non-visible, neurodiversity, and trauma. These need visual representation too.
Indigenous representation — Guide 18 requires consultation with Indigenous advisors. Do not use generic stock.
AI-generated images — AI can help but requires extra scrutiny. Check for artifacts, stereotypes, and inaccurate assistive devices. Always human-review.

7. Image Generation Strategy

How to produce ~200-300 visual assets across 18 courses efficiently.

Realistic Photos (Scenarios & People)

  • Source from: Disabled And Here, Disability:IN, Elevate by Getty
  • Supplement with: AI-generated photos (reviewed for accuracy)
  • Ideal: Custom UHN photography sessions
  • Per course: 3-5 photos (scenes + characters)
  • Total: ~60-90 unique photos
  • Reuse backgrounds across courses

Infographics & Frameworks (Concepts)

  • Style: Clean flat design matching your approved Canva references
  • Tool: Canva, Figma, or AI + manual refinement
  • 4 shared frameworks designed ONCE
  • Per course: 1-2 custom infographics
  • Total: ~40 infographic assets
  • Consistent color palette and typography

Icons & Micro-elements (Navigation)

  • ~100 icons total (20 shared + 5 per course)
  • Flat vector, 2-color, legible at 24px
  • Tool: Figma icon library or licensed set
  • Match UHN brand palette
  • Include disability-specific icons per guide
  • Consistent across all 18 courses

AI Image Generation Warning

For disability representation specifically: AI image generators have improved significantly but can still introduce inaccuracies — wrong wheelchair types, unrealistic assistive device placement, stereotypical poses, and AI artifacts (extra fingers, distorted features). Every AI-generated image depicting a person with a disability must be reviewed by someone with knowledge of that specific disability. For Guide 18 (Indigenous Peoples), do NOT use AI-generated images — use only approved stock or custom photography reviewed by Indigenous advisors.

10. Critical Insight: People as Experts, Not Subjects

The single most important design pattern from the research.

The W3C/edX Gold Standard

The W3C "Introduction to Web Accessibility" course on edX features people with disabilities as speakers, experts, and demonstrators — not as case studies or subjects being observed. People who are blind, deaf, have motor disabilities, and cognitive disabilities appear as instructors and co-teachers, showing how they use assistive technology in first-person.

This is the most impactful design decision you can make for the UHN course. Feature healthcare workers and patients with disabilities as experts sharing their experience, not as examples being discussed by someone else.

Do This
  • ✓ Person with a vision disability demonstrates how they navigate the hospital
  • ✓ Deaf healthcare worker explains communication preferences in first person
  • ✓ Person using a wheelchair shares what good accessibility looks like at a reception desk
  • ✓ Indigenous elder speaks about accessibility from their cultural perspective
  • ✓ Person with non-visible disability explains what colleagues should know
Avoid This
  • ✗ Narrator describes "challenges faced by people with disabilities" over stock footage
  • ✗ Able-bodied instructor explains disability etiquette without disabled voices
  • ✗ Stock photo of person in wheelchair looking out a window (pity framing)
  • ✗ Generic "diversity" stock showing one wheelchair user in a group
  • ✗ Simulation where non-disabled actors "experience" disability

11. Storyline 360 Implementation Specs

Translating platform design patterns into specific Storyline 360 slide masters and interactions.

Slide Masters to Build

  • Hero/Welcome: Full-bleed photo, dark gradient overlay, white title text (MasterClass pattern)
  • Content: Warm white background, left-aligned text, right image area (Coursera pattern)
  • Key Insight: Large centered serif text on dark charcoal (#1C1C1E) background (MasterClass quote pattern)
  • Scenario: Full-width photo, dialogue overlay box, choice buttons at bottom
  • Framework: Clean infographic area, consistent with your Canva references
  • Quiz: Clean card layout, 3-4 option buttons, branded submit button
  • Reflection: Calm photo background, text input field, contemplative mood
  • Resource: Card list with icons, download buttons

Interaction Patterns

  • Click-reveal tabs: 3-5 buttons along top, each triggers a slide layer (Coursera module pattern)
  • Card carousel: Prev/Next buttons cycle through content layers (Inclusive Practice Tips)
  • Step interaction: 5 numbered circles for Decision Path, click to expand each step
  • Branching scenario: 2-3 choice buttons, each triggers a consequence layer, then debrief
  • In-module check-in: 2-3 ungraded freeform questions mid-module, not just end (Udacity pattern)
  • Downloadable resource: Button triggers JavaScript to open PDF in new tab
  • Video panel: 30/70 split — small video left, interactive content right (Udacity pattern)

Player Settings

  • Sidebar: Enabled — menu + resources tab
  • Menu: Non-linear navigation allowed
  • Colors: Primary #003366, Accent #00A5A8
  • Font: UHN brand font or Inter/Source Sans Pro
  • Seekbar: On for content, off for quizzes
  • Captions: Enabled by default on all slides with audio
  • Playback speed: Adjustable (accessibility requirement)
  • Module length: 8-12 minutes max per section (MasterClass/Udacity pacing)
